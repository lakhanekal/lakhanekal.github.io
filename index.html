My Research

Postdoctoral Research on Peroxisomal Protein Import

At EMBL Hamburg, I investigated the essential role of an enigmatic matrix protein, Pex8, in peroxisomal cargo translocation. I determined Pex8's crystal structure (PDB: 8RQT) and solved its cryo-EM structure in complex with the major cycling receptor Pex5 (PDB: 9SFW; EMDB: EMD-54867). These structures revealed that Pex8 binds to Pex5 through two distinct sites, including a novel binding interface. Combined with AlphaFold3 modelling and in vivo import assays, we propose a model where this bimodal binding restricts Pex5's conformational flexibility, correctly positioning its ubiquitination site to initiate receptor recycling. This work (bioRxiv, preprint) elucidates the key transition from cargo release to receptor recycling in peroxisomal translocation. (Ref. 1)

PhD Research on Peroxisome Dynamics in Budding Yeast

At the University of Sheffield, I focused on molecular mechanisms governing fission and segregation in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. First, I discovered that the membrane protein Pex27 is concentrated at constricted sites on elongated peroxisomes and further recruits dynamin-related protein Vps1 to these sites to induce fission, thus acting as an essential receptor for Vps1. This study highlighted how process-specific receptors achieve spatial control of Dynamin-related proteins on different membranes. (Ref. 3) Subsequently, I studied the regulation of myosin motor (Myo2) and actin cytoskeleton-based organelle transport. Using high-content genetic screening, I identified spindle position checkpoint (SPOC) kinase Kin4 as a key regulator of peroxisome and vacuole transport. I demonstrated that Kin4 prevents premature Cla4- and Dma1-dependent degradation of Vac17 (vacuole-specific Myo2 receptor) in mother cells. These findings revealed a novel function for Kin4 beyond cell cycle control, showing how opposing kinase activities create distinct cellular zones to regulate organelle segregation during asymmetric cell division. (Ref. 2, 4)

Master's Research on Bacterial Stress Response

My research journey began with investigating the mycobacterial Rel enzyme, critical for (p)ppGpp metabolism. I identified and characterised an Intrinsically Disordered Region (IDR) of conserved length in Rel that drives the C-terminal ACT domain movement to control the switch between the enzyme's (p)ppGpp synthesis and hydrolysis activities. I found conserved motifs within this IDR, later shown to be nucleotide-binding sites, that govern this molecular switch. This work provided key mechanistic insights into how pathogenic bacteria adapt to environmental stress, laying the foundation for my interest in fundamental cellular regulation. (Ref. 6)

Future Perspectives: The Host-Pathogen Nexus

Building on my expertise in peroxisome biology and structural mechanisms, my future independent research programme will pivot to the host-pathogen nexus. Peroxisomes are increasingly recognised as critical hubs for the innate immune response and are targeted by various pathogens (e.g., viruses and bacteria) to subvert host defences. The goal is to apply an integrative structural and cell biology approach to:

  • Identify novel pathogen effector proteins that specifically target peroxisomal components (PMPs and matrix proteins).
  • Determine the cryo-EM structures of key host-pathogen complexes (e.g., Pex-effector complexes) to uncover the molecular basis of immune evasion.
  • Develop chemical or genetic modulators that interfere with pathogen-mediated manipulation of peroxisomal function, paving the way for new therapeutic strategies against infectious diseases.
This research will bridge the gap between organelle dynamics and infectious disease, establishing a unique and timely research direction.

    This manuscript reveals the first structure of the essential Pex8-Pex5 receptor complex. Combining in vivo assays and computational analysis, we provide insights into the mechanisms of cargo release and receptor recycling, which are critical for peroxisomal matrix protein import.

  2. Ekal L#, Alqahtani AMS, Ayscough KR, Hettema EH#, Spatiotemporal regulation of organelle transport by spindle position checkpoint kinase Kin4. 2024, J Cell Sci, # Co-corresponding author, Selected as a Research Highlight; 137 (21): e137_e2102) DOI: 10.1242/jcs.261948

    This paper deciphers the antagonistic interplay between spindle position checkpoint (SPOC) kinase Kin4 and p21 activated kinase (PAK) Cla4, establishing a novel mechanism that regulates the distribution of peroxisomes and vacuoles. These findings highlight conserved principles for organelle segregation during asymmetric cell growth and division.

  3. Ekal L, Alqahtani AMS, Hettema EH, The dynamin-related protein Vps1 and the peroxisomal membrane protein Pex27 function together during peroxisome fission. 2023, J Cell Sci, Selected as a Research Highlight; 136 (6): e136_e0601 DOI: 10.1242/jcs.246348

    This work reveals Pex27 (a peroxisomal membrane protein) as an essential receptor for the dynamin-related protein Vps1 during peroxisome fission. We show that Pex27 concentrates at constricted sites on elongated peroxisomes to recruit Vps1 to induce fission, highlighting how process-specific receptors achieve spatial control of dynamin-related proteins.

  4. Ekal L, Alqahtani AMS, Zalckvar E, Schuldiner M, Hettema EH, Ayscough KR, Spindle Position Checkpoint Kinase Kin4 Regulates Organelle Transport in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. 2023, Biomolecules, DOI: 10.3390/biom13071098

    We report a novel function for the kinase Kin4 in regulating peroxisome and vacuole transport, moving beyond its known role in cell cycle regulation. This study also uncovers Frk1 as a functional paralogue to Kin4, revealing its involvement in organelle transport and suggesting a role as a putative checkpoint kinase.

  5. Ekal L, Hettema EH, Targeted Modifications of the Yeast Genome to Study Peroxisomes. 2023, Meth Mol Biol, DOI: 10.1007/978-1-0716-3048-8_16

    This book chapter provides detailed protocols for targeted genome modifications in yeast, offering a practical guide for researchers studying peroxisomes. It serves as a valuable resource for manipulating the yeast genome to investigate peroxisome biology.

The conserved motifs within this IDR are nucleotide-binding sites that govern this precise regulatory mechanism.
